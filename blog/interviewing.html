Interviewing mistakes your startup is making

This is the first article in a series where I vent my strong opinions on running engineering interviews. Here, I'll talk about common mistakes. In parts 2 and 3 I'll compare different interview formats and then do a deep dive into an example interview flow.

There's this endless lamentation from engineers of how painful it is to interview for a new job. You set aside multiple days for the process, solve silly whiteboarding problems and are met with a frustratingly generic rejection letter if you don't get an offer.

For early-stage startups in particular, very little thought is put into designing an interview process. The payoff for putting in time isn't immediately obvious and you have so many other things to get done. So you cargo cult interviews you've endured in the past. It's almost like hazing — you suffered through it, so any worthy software engineer should be able to as well.

I, like many others, hate this process and as a new manager saw this as my new crusade to wage. But I quickly realized that interviewing sucks for everyone and that there's no quick and easy fix to it. That doesn't mean that you should give up. With careful consideration (as well as iteration) you can both mitigate pain for everyone involved and adequately vet candidates.
